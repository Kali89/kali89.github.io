_"Children will be given back their childhoods thanks to government action to
ban social media platforms from offering services to under-16s, with_ ***less time
for scrolling*** _and_ ***more time for play.***_"_

The Government's bold new policy to ban social media (and livestreaming, and
YouTube, and some games) shows a lack of precision on the harms it is looking
to prevent, the outcomes it is hoping for, and the implementation details.
Without a specific problem to target, the proposed solution is far more
all-encompasing than necessary.

Let's consider parts of the DSIT announcement and be precise about them:

## Choosing a Harm

### 1. Less time for scrolling and more time for play.

This suggests that ***total time*** is the key metric: right now we believe
that children are spending too much time on social media and that, if we
removed that time, they'd spend it on play instead.

The first part isn't controversial - teenagers today spend N hours/day on
social media (replicated across multiple studies). What teenagers would do if
they **weren't** on social media is absolutely not clear. Whether _play_
includes video games isn't specified. Whether TV counts as _play_ isn't clear.

The action that you'd take if your goal was to reduce the amount of time
children spend scrolling is almost certainly not a ban on specific platforms. Time limits per platform or per device would be more direct. Platforms could work to encourage children to spend less time without outright bans.

### 2. User-to-user platforms whose purpose is to enable social interaction and which allow users to post material, alongside algorithms.

I included this just because it's funny in its lack of precision. This implies
that **the purpose** of a platform is relevant in the harm it causes, which is
clearly nonsense. _Alongside algorithms_ is similarly non-specific: is content
being amplified by algorithmic means the problem? Or is it simply the existence
of algorithms on a platform that causes some kind of harm?

Really, this is interesting insofar as it **doesn't** talk about time and
instead suggests that it's something about the platform itself that is causing
harm.

### 3. World-leading blocks on harmful functions such as livestreaming and stranger communication with children

Editor notes clarify that livestreaming will be for under-16s livestreaming
**themselves**, and that stranger communications includes all methods for
_"unknown users to contact and talk with children"_.

Again, neither (explicitly) related to time spent: under 16s are no longer
allowed to talk to people that they don't know online. Both of these appear
designed to guard against specific harms, and both of these harms appear
unrelated to the amount of time people spend on social media.

Whether we want to enshrine the notion that even talking to strangers at a
distance of 3000 miles is dangerous is a choice for the Government. Whether we
can feasibly know who is a stranger to any given 16 year old is a problem for
somebody else at the moment.

### 4. Overnight curfews and breaks in infinite scrolling for under-18-year-olds

Now we're talking more specifically about time-related harms - with overnight
curfews suggesting that the time-of-day that children spend online is related
to the harm they experience. This is not at all founded in academic literature
thus far. Breaks in infinite scrolling are a mechanism that _could_ reduce the
amount of time people spend on social media.

Here we see the Government outright stating what they think the answer should
be in order to reduce people's time, rather than relying on the platforms
themselves to understand the levers for which people's time can be best
reduced.


### 5. Real-time content makes harmful material harder to moderate, and algorithmic feeds can intensify exposure to dangerous, distressing or overly engaging material.

The first part of this is perhaps the reason for the ban on livestreaming: the
Government believes that tech companies are incapable of moderating real-time
content and is thus proposing an outright ban. The part on algorithmic feeds is
again pointing to the design of the platform itself as being harmful, rather
than the time spent.

Presumably, rather than trying to devise a list of all content that might be
considered _"overly engaging"_, the Government has sought to ban algorithmic
feeds for children instead. Realising that all feeds are algorithmic, they have
decided to focus on the _"intent"_ of the platform containing an algorithmic
feed, as a way to ban the things it wants to, without having to spell out the
reasons for the ban.
