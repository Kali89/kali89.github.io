As I've previously mentioned, I gave a talk yesterday at the Oxford Internet
Institute about phone use, and whether or not it's habitual (spoiler: it is).
In writing that talk, I ended up thinking a little more about psychological
discomfort and I thought it'd be worth fleshing those thoughts out a little
more.<!--more-->

## Dual-Process Theory

Let's start with a little psychological theory. Dual-process theory states that
there are two different cognitive processes responsible for our actions and
perceptions:

**System 1** is a quick, automatic and effortless system that directs our
actions without incurring any real cognitive load. When you're acting on
autopilot, mindlessly opening your phone or driving your car or otherwise going
about your day, you're using system 1.

**System 2** is the slow and deliberate version of you: the one that plans and
decides and reflects. This is the system that says *"maybe we should get
healthy and take up running?"* or *"maybe moving to Sweden is a good idea"*.

## Mental Discomfort

One of the questions I opened my talk with yesterday was supposedly a simple
one: *"How many times will you check your phone tomorrow?"*

I asked the question because I was hoping to cause a little discomfort -
asking the question of myself certainly makes me a little uncomfortable. I
asked those at the talk to reflect on **why** that simple question makes them
feel uncomfortable.

My views are as follows:

## Self-Image

System 2 controls how we see ourselves. Am I kind? Ambitious? Loyal? An
intellectual? A tech worker?

Ultimately I have mental image of myself and it's a relatively high-brow,
optimisitic image consisting of all the best things that I do and all the big
decisions I take. I'm a father and a husband. I'm a staff data scientist. I'm
an academic. I live in the countryside.

However, System 1 directs a lot of my behaviour and actions. I drink a coffee
every morning because otherwise I get a headache. I have diverticular disease
so can't drink too much coffee. I spend a lot of time on Reddit. Of an evening
I like to tune out by watching whatever my wife wants to watch on TV.

These are all facts about me, but I don't view them as a big part of my
self-image. They're just things I do.

Why does asking about my phone habits evoke such psychological discomfort,
then?

## Colliding Systems

My theory (and it's just that, a theory) is that asking a question about my
phone use forces my system 2 to consider and comprehend the work of my system
1. My research shows pretty conclusively that phone use is largely a function
   of system 1. Yet my self image is built and sustained by system 2.

So when I'm asked to recollect and reflect on my phone use, I'm starkly forced
to **truly** reflect on who I am, and how I spend my time. And with so much of
my time going on my phone, I don't perhaps like what I see. I don't like the
fact that I spend hours a day browsing Reddit and Twitter - that doesn't form
part of my self-image. And yet...

## Steering into Discomfort

My system 2 knows that I spend too long on my phone and should do other things.
But it can usually ignore that fact, and I can go on imagining myself to be
some paragon of human virtue. Well, maybe not quite, but at least a bit better
than I actually am.

Questions that produce an uncomfortable feeling, I think, do so because they
force me to acknowledge my shortcomings. They force me to name them, and in
some cases to quantify how I'm personally failing.

Psychologically, those feelings seem like they're the perfect place to begin
studying. I've been thinking a lot recently about Tolstoy's views on "What
drives men to stupefy themselves" (with alcohol), and I'm 100% going to write a
post soon on social media's new place as alcohol for the masses.

## Conclusion

Being asked to state what you've done in a day, and to forecast what you're
going to do tomorrow shouldn't be a difficult thing that brings you
psychological discomfort. If it does, that suggests that you're doing/going to
do something that you know you shouldn't.

One person doing this once, I think, isn't unusual. But an entire generation
doing this every day with respect to their mobile phones...well, that sounds
pretty rough.
