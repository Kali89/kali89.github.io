> _"Children will be given back their childhoods thanks to government action to ban social media platforms from offering services to under-16s, with less time for scrolling and more time for play."_

The Government's announcement that it will ban social media for under-16s is really two announcements disguised as one. Firstly,  a set of restrictions on particular functions including stranger contact, livestreaming, infinite scroll etc. Secondly, the ban: booting under-16s off social media platforms entirely. These are not the same kind of policy. The first picks out specific features and limits them; the second removes the whole thing. Taken together, these make little sense.

For now at least, let us grant the Government its functional restrictions, even though several of these "harms" do not survive much scrutiny. "Stranger communication" is not a harm; it is a function, one through which a real harm can (rarely) occur. Talking to strangers is not inherently harmful. "Livestreaming" is a format that can be good or bad. And "overly engaging material", whether delivered via algorithmic feed or otherwise, is bad by definition: something is overly engaging precisely when it holds attention longer than we would like, which is only to say that consumes the time we were already worried about. Where the announcement does point at genuinely serious harms (grooming, child sexual abuse material) it points at things that are already crimes, already covered by the Online Safety Act's illegal-content duties, and no more or less illegal whether a child is on the platform for ten minutes or ten hours. A ban adds nothing to their illegality.
Ignore those problems, though. Once we have set the genuine harms aside as already handled, and grant every functional restriction the Government wants, we are then left with a very important question: once the functions are restricted, what is the ban *for*?

The package, the DSIT press release says, is "one that responds to how children experience harm online, rather than just where it happens". The functional restrictions are the *how*. The ban is the *where*. Once you have addressed the how (eliminating stranger contact, livestreaming, infinite scroll and the rest) the only thing the ban adds is removal by location. The justification left over, after everything else has been accounted for, is the line in the very first paragraph: less time for scrolling, more time for play. The purpose of the ban is time.

## If time is the concern, regulate time

So suppose time is the concern. It is a serious one, and I don't want to be flippant about it. The unease about how many hours teenagers (and the rest of us!) pour into social media is real, widely felt, and not obviously wrong. But to many of the older generation, it may sound a little familiar. Lost hours, passivity, the displacement of play and homework and conversation; the very same complaints attached to television and a generation threatened with developing square eyes. Being uncomfortable with the time is not, by itself, a policy.

If time were the stated concern, a total ban would be a strange way to regulate. The tool that matches a time concern is clearly a limit. In legislating against specific mechanisms (infinite scroll, mandated breaks, no late nights), the Government  has it backwards. If the goal is fewer hours, the regulator does not need to know or care *how* a platform delivers them. Set the outcome and let the firms find the best way to hit it. If infinite scroll has to go to meet the target, the platform will remove infinite scroll; it understands its own product far better than the department does. This is the difference between a performance standard and a design standard. We price carbon rather than mandating particular boilers precisely because the regulator should set the target and stay out of the engineering. Here the Government is doing the engineering and leaving the target — the hours — strangely unstated.

## Would a cap actually bite?

According to Ofcom, the average thirteen and fourteen year old is online for
around four hours each day, and the average is dragged upwards by a long tail of
extremely heavy users. A social media cap set near the average of total online time therefore, leaves the moderate majority largely untouched and instead tackles the extreme cases where
most concern lies.

It is there where the evidence, such as it is, points to a problem being. The cleaner findings put the inflection at around three hours a day, with moderate use indistinguishable from (and sometimes better than!) minimal use, and heavier use associated with worse outcomes. A three-hour cap is therefore not a cosmetic figure plucked from the air; it is roughly the line the research itself draws, acting as a ceiling on the few rather than a prohibition on the many. A more proportionate response is something that a blanket ban cannot manage: a fifteen-year-old who spends twenty minutes a day on YouTube is not the same as one spending six hours on TikTok. Blanket bans have no concept of dose at all, despite dose being the entire substance of the time complaint.

Of course, a cap isn't a panacea. It does nothing about illegal content, but nor should it. Existing criminal law and illegal-content duties are precisely for this, and they bind regardless of how long anyone is logged on. Where a cap makes sense is on the one thing the announcement highlights but otherwise fails to pin down: the hours. For that, it is the proportionate tool. The harder problem it shares with the ban is enforcement, and on enforcement, the last two years are instructive.

## We have already run this experiment

When the Online Safety Act's age checks went live in July 2025, VPN use exploded. Ofcom's count of daily VPN users jumped from a pre-Act baseline of around 650,000 to more than 1.4 million within weeks, and at least one provider reported a fourteen-fold surge. Undeterred, the state's response to circumvention has been to move against the circumvention tool and seek to regulate VPN usage. The same Bill carries the under-16 social media ban. So in a single legislative pass the Government is banning teenagers from the platforms and moving to wall off the tool they would use to get around the ban. We can only hope that the next evasion, and the next law chasing it, are already lined up. Enforcing a prohibition against motivated, capable teenagers is an escalating series of checks upon checks, each one eroding a little privacy and liberty for all, ultimately to be circumvented in turn. While a time cap inherits the same arms race, it inherits no more of it than the ban does, while asking for a great deal less.

## Even if it works

That teenagers will evade a ban is well-evidenced. Australia's own regulator concedes that a substantial share of under-16s have kept or remade their accounts since its ban came into force, and others have catalogued the workarounds at length. But for a moment let's ignore that and instead assume the ban works exactly as written and every under-16 is genuinely off. The promise is more time for play but is that realistic? What actually fills the freed hours?

The most likely destinations are another screen, whether the broadcast television and streaming that an earlier generation's anxieties were about or, more likely, existing on-demand streaming platforms and whatever else pops up in the wake of a successful ban. For some, it may be previous anxieties: the unsupervised activities an older moral panic targeted. Play, the advertised outcome, is well down that list, and the announcement offers nothing to suggest otherwise. If you are going to remove the central social channel of an entire age group, the burden is on you to show that what replaces it is better. "Childhood" restored is asserted, neither demonstrated nor even likely, and lest we forget, this assumes perfect enforcement.

## You cannot regulate a harm you cannot measure

There is a crucial question that the ban is not answering. What precisely is the harm that it is aiming to protect from? Gestures are made at wellbeing, at childhood, and at "overly engaging" everything...but none of those things survive any kind of measurement, and behavioural evidence that lies beneath these kinds of measures relies heavily upon self-reports which are famously unreliable. The gap between what people say they do on their phones and what they actually do is wide enough to drive this entire debate through. 

A time cap is, just like a complete ban, the state telling teenagers how to spend their free time. However, the distinction is not between freedom and paternalism. It is between an intervention with a measurable aim and one with none. You can check whether a cap is working: people either spend less time on social media, or they do not. You can check whether functional bans are working: teenagers can either talk to strangers or they cannot. You cannot check whether a childhood has been handed back, because no one has said what would count as evidence that it had. Two years from now, on the Government's own framing, there will be no way to say whether the ban is doing what the Government hopes it will.

The social media ban is overreaching, theoretically rendered pointless by
functional bans that are already proposed, and gives no way of ultimately
measuring its own success.
